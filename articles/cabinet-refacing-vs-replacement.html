Cabinet Refacing vs Full Replacement in Vancouver: Which Option Is Right for You?

RSN Kitchen Cabinets Ltd. Updated April 2026 Metro Vancouver & BC

When your kitchen cabinets look dated or worn, you have two main options: refacing them (replacing the doors and visible surfaces while keeping the existing boxes) or replacing them entirely. Both are valid approaches, but choosing the wrong one can mean either overspending or getting less than you need. Here's how to think through the decision.

What Is Cabinet Refacing?

Cabinet refacing involves keeping your existing cabinet carcasses in place and replacing only the visible elements — the doors, drawer fronts, and exposed end panels. The box interiors remain as-is. Hinges and hardware are typically replaced at the same time.

A high-quality refacing job can produce a kitchen that looks completely transformed without the demolition, disposal, and lead time of a full replacement. The kitchen is livable throughout the process, typically taking just a few days.

Refacing makes sense when the existing boxes are still in good structural condition — no water damage, no warping, no signs of pest activity — and when the kitchen layout is exactly what you want.

What Does Full Cabinet Replacement Involve?

Full replacement means removing all existing cabinetry down to the bare walls and installing entirely new cabinets — new boxes, new doors, new hardware, everything. This is typically paired with new countertops and often backsplash, as the entire kitchen is essentially reset.

Replacement takes longer (2–5 days for cabinet installation alone, plus countertop lead time), creates more disruption, and costs more — but it also gives you a completely fresh start with new materials, new sizing, and new layout options if desired.

Choose Refacing When...

Refacing Makes Sense

  • Your existing cabinet boxes are solid and undamaged
  • You love your kitchen layout and don't want to change it
  • You want a significant visual transformation at lower cost
  • You want minimal kitchen downtime
  • You just need updated doors/style, not new storage
  • You're planning to sell the home within 5–10 years

Replacement Makes Sense

  • Existing boxes have water damage, warping, or structural failure
  • You want to change the kitchen layout or sizing
  • The existing boxes are particleboard and are deteriorating
  • You want to maximize storage with new interior accessories
  • You're doing a full renovation and want everything new
  • This is a long-term home where longevity matters

The Condition of Your Existing Boxes Is the Key Question

The most important factor is the condition of your current cabinet carcasses. Before committing to refacing, inspect the boxes carefully:

  • Water damage: Check under the sink and around the dishwasher. Swollen, soft, or discoloured carcass panels are a sign of past leaks. Refacing damaged boxes will result in doors that don't align and hinges that don't hold.
  • Particleboard degradation: Older BC homes often have particleboard cabinet boxes from the 1980s–1990s. If these boxes are delaminating or structurally soft at edges and joints, refacing is a waste of money — the boxes themselves need replacement.
  • Layout limitations: Refacing cannot move the sink, add an island, or change where doors open. If you're frustrated with the kitchen's layout, refacing won't solve it.

Cost Comparison in Metro Vancouver

For a medium-size kitchen (12–16 linear feet of cabinetry):

  • Cabinet refacing: $8,000–$18,000 depending on door material, finish, and scope
  • Full custom replacement: $22,000–$45,000 for the same footprint

Refacing is typically 40–60% of the cost of full replacement. However, if your existing boxes are particleboard or have any structural issues, you'll end up replacing them anyway — at greater total cost than if you had replaced everything from the start. Have an experienced contractor assess the boxes before committing to refacing.

What About Painting Existing Cabinets?

Some homeowners consider painting their existing cabinet doors and boxes as a lower-cost alternative to both refacing and replacement. A quality paint job by a skilled finisher can look good and typically costs $3,000–$8,000 for a full kitchen. However, painted cabinets are prone to chipping at corners, and the result is always limited by the quality of the existing doors. If your current doors are dated in profile or have routed decorative details you dislike, painting doesn't change the shape — only the colour.
