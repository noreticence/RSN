Kitchen Cabinet Materials Guide: Choosing the Right Wood for BC Homes

RSN Kitchen Cabinets Ltd. Updated April 2026 British Columbia

The materials you choose for your kitchen cabinets affect how they look, how long they last, and how much they cost. This guide covers the main wood species used for cabinet doors, carcass materials, and finish options — with practical guidance for Canadian homeowners making these choices for the first time.

The Two Parts of a Cabinet: Box and Door

Every cabinet has two distinct material decisions: the carcass (the box) and the door and drawer fronts. They're often made from different materials and each has its own set of options.

Carcass Materials: Plywood vs MDF vs Particleboard

Plywood

Plywood is the premium choice for cabinet carcasses and the material RSN uses for all custom work. It's strong, stable, holds screws and fasteners well, and handles moisture better than alternatives. In a kitchen environment where steam, spills, and humidity are a reality, plywood's cross-grain construction resists warping and swelling over time.

Well-made plywood carcasses in a custom kitchen can last 30–40 years with normal use. They're also more repairable — damage to a single panel doesn't mean replacing the whole run.

MDF (Medium-Density Fiberboard)

MDF is denser and smoother than plywood, which makes it excellent for painted flat-panel doors and cabinet components where a flawless, grain-free surface is needed. It machines precisely and takes paint exceptionally well. However, MDF is heavier than plywood, doesn't hold screws as reliably (especially at edges), and is highly susceptible to moisture — a real concern in Canadian kitchens where humidity swings seasonally.

MDF is commonly used for door cores and painted drawer fronts, where its smooth surface is an advantage, but RSN uses plywood for carcasses to ensure structural longevity.

Particleboard

Particleboard is the least expensive option and is common in flat-pack furniture and entry-level stock cabinets. It's not suitable for quality custom cabinetry — it's heavy, holds fasteners poorly, and degrades quickly in humid environments. If you're investing in custom work, you should expect and insist on plywood carcasses.

Plywood Pros

  • Excellent screw-holding strength
  • Moisture-resistant structure
  • Lightweight relative to strength
  • 30–40 year lifespan
  • Repairable if damaged

MDF Best Uses

  • Painted flat-panel doors
  • Painted drawer fronts
  • Decorative components
  • Smooth paint finishes
  • Interior components (low moisture)

Wood Species for Cabinet Doors and Faces

The wood species used for doors, drawer fronts, and exposed frames determines the aesthetic and price of the cabinet. Here are the most common species used in BC:

Maple

Maple is the most widely used hardwood for kitchen cabinets in Canada. It has a fine, tight grain with minimal variation — excellent for painted finishes because the surface is smooth and consistent. It also takes stain well, though the tight grain means colour can be less dramatic than open-grain species. Maple is durable, relatively affordable compared to premium species, and available in consistent quality. An ideal all-purpose species for most kitchen projects.

White Oak

White oak has surged in popularity over the last several years — it's the wood most associated with contemporary and transitional kitchen design in BC. Its distinctive ray fleck pattern, visible in quarter-sawn cuts, gives it a unique visual character that's increasingly sought-after in high-end homes. White oak takes natural and medium stains beautifully and has excellent durability. It costs more than maple but is worth the premium for the look it delivers.

Alder

Alder is a softer hardwood (technically a hardwood, but softer than maple or oak) that's popular in rustic and transitional kitchens. It takes stain evenly and has a warm, characterful grain. Because it's softer, it's slightly more prone to dents and dings over time, but for most homeowners the warmth of the appearance is worth it. Alder is generally less expensive than maple or oak, making it a good choice for budget-conscious custom work.

Walnut

Walnut is a premium wood used in high-end custom kitchens. Its rich, dark chocolate tones with striking grain variation make it one of the most visually impressive cabinet materials available. Walnut is typically used as a natural or lightly oiled finish — staining walnut tends to obscure what makes it beautiful. It's considerably more expensive than maple or oak, and grain matching across a full kitchen adds to the fabrication time and cost. For luxury projects, there's nothing quite like it.

Cherry

Cherry is a classic choice for traditional and transitional kitchens. It has a fine, smooth grain and a warm reddish-brown tone that deepens and enriches over time with exposure to light — a characteristic called "patina" that some homeowners love and others need to be aware of. Cherry is slightly less common than it was 15–20 years ago as tastes have shifted toward lighter woods, but it remains a beautiful and durable choice.

Painted MDF (No Wood Grain)

Many homeowners want a clean, white or solid-colour kitchen with no visible grain. In this case, the best door material is MDF with a quality lacquer or paint finish. The result is a flawlessly smooth surface with rich, even colour. Painted finishes are popular in contemporary and transitional kitchens and are often combined with plywood carcasses. The limitation is that painted surfaces are more susceptible to chipping at corners — quality of the finish coat matters significantly.

Hardware: An Underrated Decision

Hinges, drawer slides, and pulls affect the day-to-day feel of a kitchen as much as any aesthetic choice. Specify soft-close hinges and full-extension, under-mount drawer slides at minimum. For drawer boxes, dovetail-jointed solid wood or high-quality plywood construction is significantly more durable than the stamped-metal drawer boxes common in semi-custom lines.

BC Climate Note British Columbia has a wide range of climates — coastal Vancouver and Victoria see significant humidity swings, while Kelowna and the Okanagan see hot, dry summers and cold winters. Custom cabinets built with plywood carcasses and properly acclimatized solid wood components handle these conditions well. Always let new cabinets acclimatize to the home before final adjustments.
