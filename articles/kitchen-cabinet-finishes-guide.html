Choosing Kitchen Cabinet Finishes & Door Styles in BC

RSN Kitchen Cabinets Ltd. Updated April 2026 British Columbia

The door style and finish of your kitchen cabinets define the look and feel of the entire kitchen more than any other single decision. Get this right and everything else falls into place. Get it wrong and no amount of nice countertops or hardware will compensate. Here's a practical guide to making these choices confidently for a BC home renovation.

Cabinet Door Styles

Door profile is the starting point. The style you choose should suit both your personal taste and the architectural character of your home.

Most Popular

Shaker

A five-piece door with a flat centre panel and simple square frame. Clean, versatile, works in contemporary, transitional, and traditional kitchens. The dominant style in BC right now and has been for a decade — for good reason. Can be painted or stained.

Contemporary

Flat Panel (Slab)

A single flat panel with no frame or profile — pure minimalism. Popular in modern and Scandinavian-influenced kitchens. Usually painted or lacquered for a seamless, grain-free look. Works especially well in open-plan homes where the kitchen is part of the living space.

Traditional

Raised Panel

A five-piece door with a raised centre panel and detailed profile. Traditional and formal — common in heritage-style BC homes and Victorian-era renovations. Less popular in new construction but still the right choice for the right home.

Current Trend

Shaker with Integrated Handle

A variation on the shaker where the bottom rail is extended or routed to create a finger-pull, eliminating separate hardware. Very clean look, common in contemporary BC kitchens. Often combined with slab drawer fronts on lower cabinets.

Distinctive

Beaded Inset

Doors that sit inside the cabinet frame (rather than overlaying it) with a beaded detail on the frame face. More complex to build, more precise to install — requires exacting craftsmanship. Beautiful and distinctive. Found in high-end transitional and traditional kitchens.

Open Feel

Glass Front

Upper cabinet doors with glass panels — either clear, reeded, or frosted. Used selectively to break up a solid bank of upper cabinets and display dishware. Glass fronts require the cabinet interior to be finished (painted or veneered) and lit if displayed prominently.

Finish Options

The finish is applied to the door (and often the carcass exterior) and determines the colour, sheen, and texture of the cabinet surface. There are three main categories:

Painted Finish

A solid-colour painted finish (white, off-white, navy, sage green, greige, black) is the most popular choice in Metro Vancouver for both shaker and slab door styles. Paint completely covers the wood grain, producing a uniform colour surface. The quality of a painted finish depends heavily on the primer, preparation, and number of coats — a properly done painted cabinet should be smooth, hard, and resistant to yellowing.

Paint on cabinet doors is typically applied as a conversion varnish or two-component lacquer in a spray environment, not brushed on like wall paint. The resulting surface is harder and more durable than wall paint and can be cleaned with mild cleaners. The main limitation is chipping at corners — high-traffic kitchens will show wear at edges over years of use.

Stained Finish

Staining enhances the natural grain of the wood while adding colour. A light stain on white oak shows off the ray fleck pattern. A medium walnut stain on maple adds warmth. Dark ebonized stains on oak create dramatic contrast. Stained finishes are protected with a topcoat (typically lacquer or polyurethane) that determines the sheen level.

Stained cabinets show the natural character of the wood — which is exactly the point. They also show fingerprints less than high-gloss painted cabinets, an advantage in active kitchens. Repairs are more involved than painted finishes since matching a stain requires matching the wood grain too.

Natural / Clear Finish

A clear coat on bare wood — lacquer, oil, or hardwax — lets the natural wood tone come through completely. Common with walnut (where the natural colour is the feature) and increasingly with white oak in Scandinavian-influenced kitchen designs. Natural-finished cabinets warm and patina over time — some homeowners love this, others should be aware of it before choosing.

Two-Tone Kitchens

Two-tone kitchens — where upper and lower cabinets are different colours or finishes — have become increasingly popular in BC over the past several years. Common combinations:

  • White uppers / navy or forest green lowers
  • White uppers / natural wood island
  • Painted perimeter / stained natural oak island
  • Light upper cabinets / darker base cabinets to visually ground the kitchen

Two-tone works best when the combination is deliberate — not random. The island as a contrasting element is a particularly clean approach because it creates a clear visual hierarchy without the upper and lower cabinets competing.

Tip from RSN If you're uncertain about colour, specify a neutral for your cabinet doors — white, off-white, or a natural wood — and put the colour into your backsplash, countertop, or accessories. These are easier and cheaper to change later than repainting cabinets.
